Human Tiger Conflict

HTC is a serious public health issue in Sundarban Reserve Forest, India. ​Beacause of the HTC we can see a significant mortality and morbidity of both human and tiger population.​One of the main reason behind it could be the increase in number of tigers straying into the surrounding villages.​ This creates a serious hazard for humans, livestock and tigers living in this type of vegetation. Confusing riverside plantations with jungles or confusing paddy fields with wild grasses (31.12 % combined) and predation of domestic livestock (26.96 %) are two main reasons for tigers straying.​ Tigers may prefer domestic livestock over wild boar and deer as hunting livestock is easier than hunting natural prey in mangroves.​​

Tigers have killed more people than any other cat.​ Only in The Sundarbans we may have highest number of human fatalities caused by tiger attacks​. A total of 3,615 human deaths were recorded in Sundarbans between the years 1881 to 2006​. A total of 1,259 tiger deaths were also recorded from 1881 to 2006.​ The Tigers population decrease have Others reasons also responsible instead of just HTC. Poaching and change in environment is one of the main reasons responsible behind it instead of just HTC.

Strategies to Reduce HTC

Several actions have been taken to minimize human-tiger conflicts inside forests such as stopping coupe operations, closing of issuance of permits for Phoenix palludosa and Nypa fruticans leaf collection, digging of fresh water ponds and re-excavation annually, introduction of human face masks and clay models wrapped with energizers which are connected to 230 Volt and 12 Volt battery sources. But none of these methods have conclusively proved to be effective.After trials and errors below methods are found to be more effective.

  1. Quick response to combat tiger straying
  2. Payment of compensation and raising awareness campaign
  3. Erection of nylon net fence
  4. Eco-development activities

Conclusion

Tigers straying from forests onto agricultural areas cause significant loss of livestock and human lives. Ignoring this fact does not help in the conservation of tiger or the Sundarban mangrove ecosystem. Retribution killing of tigers can cause serious damage to the ecosystem, by removing top predators and altering predator–prey relationships. Damage caused by tigers killing livestock is often patchily distributed with most losses accruing to a minority of stakeholders. However, when losses do occur, they can be serious, even catastrophic in economic terms. This reinforces negative attitudes among stakeholders. Costs always exceeded benefits from the view point of forest users. This justifies people intolerance for tigers. The onus to win over the people affected by depredations and to change their mindset by providing something tangible in exchange of protecting the wildlife lies with the Forest Department.
